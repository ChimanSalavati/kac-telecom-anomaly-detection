\begin{abstract}
Reliable anomaly detection is a practical bottleneck for 5G/Open RAN observability, where hundreds of heterogeneous KPIs, severe class imbalance, and strict alert budgets make purely numerical scores brittle.
We present KAC (KPI-Aware Contrastive), a shadow-integrated framework that turns compact operator-style summaries into a learnable feedback signal over full-KPI temporal evidence.
This gives the detector broad numerical coverage like modern time-series models, while retaining the sparse KPI-specific reasoning that makes operator feedback sustainable in an operational workflow.
Across public telecom telemetry, enterprise-scale Open RAN observability data, and packet-derived KPI traces with injected anomalies from Nokia's lab pipeline, KAC improves or matches strong residual-only, deep MTSAD, and zero-shot LLM baselines.
At a fixed $2.5\%$ false-positive operating regime on the public 5G benchmark, it improves recall while remaining stable under severe class imbalance.
KAC is integrated in \emph{pre-production shadow mode} with Nokia's GKE-based KPI construction pipeline, where it scores cached telemetry windows for offline comparison; it does not yet drive operator-facing alerting or mitigation.
Code, processed public benchmarks, and evaluation scripts are released at \url{https://github.com/ChimanSalavati/kac-telecom-anomaly-detection}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

5G and Open RAN observability pipelines collect heterogeneous radio, platform, and packet-level KPI streams at fine temporal granularity. These streams feed AIOps workflows for alerting, incident management, and diagnosis, yet noisy alert streams remain a persistent operational pain point~\cite{yu2024alertsurvey,bhukar2024dynamic,zhang2024aiopsllm}. The challenge is amplified in Open RAN, where disaggregated and virtualized components require anomaly detectors to jointly reason over RAN and platform telemetry~\cite{sun2024spotlight}. Recent telecom observability benchmarks further show that such data are heterogeneous, scale-sensitive, and useful beyond forecasting, including anomaly detection, root-cause analysis, and multimodal reasoning~\cite{feng2025telecomts}. We target the applied problem of producing reliable anomaly scores from noisy, high-dimensional telecom KPI windows under strict false-alarm constraints.

Multivariate time-series anomaly detection (MTSAD) has been studied extensively~\cite{schmidl2022evaluation,darban2024deep}, with deep models learning numerical representations of normal temporal behavior and inter-variable structure~\cite{su2019omnianomaly,audibert2020usad,tuli2022tranad,xu2022anomaly,deng2021gdn,zhao2020mtadgat}. However, these methods typically treat variables as numerical channels, graph nodes, or attention patterns rather than as named KPIs with operational meaning. This matters in telecom observability: the same numerical deviation can be benign or actionable depending on which KPI moved, its absolute level, and the surrounding operating regime~\cite{feng2025telecomts}.

Time-series foundation models (TSFMs), including Chronos/Chronos-2~\cite{ansari2024chronos,ansari2025chronos2}, MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment}, Moirai~\cite{woo2024moirai}, and TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}, provide strong pretrained temporal models and naturally suggest residual- or embedding-based detectors. Yet recent evidence shows that shallow forecasting-error or reconstruction-error reuse is often insufficient for anomaly detection~\cite{zhu2026oneliners}. In telecom settings, residual-only TSFM detectors are especially fragile in two recurring cases. \textbf{Context extrapolation}: if an anomalous regime already begins inside the forecasting context, a strong forecaster may extrapolate it and produce small one-step residuals even for an abnormal window. \textbf{Operating-context erasure}: residualization can suppress absolute KPI level; for example, a stable RSRP series may be forecast accurately even though its absolute level is essential for interpreting BLER, throughput, or packet-level indicators. These failure modes motivate combining full-KPI residual evidence with compact KPI-aware context rather than relying on forecast error alone.

We present \textbf{KAC} (KPI-Aware Contrastive), a shadow-integrated anomaly detector for telecom KPI streams. KAC keeps full-KPI Chronos-2 residual and interval-width features in the numerical branch, while a compact operator-style text branch summarizes salient KPI-window context under a token budget. Learnable KPI queries extract KPI-specific text representations, a shared residual projector produces matched KPI-level residual representations, and a KPI-level contrastive objective aligns the two views. Optional uncertainty weighting uses Chronos-2 prediction-interval width to reduce the influence of low-confidence residual evidence. At inference time, KAC outputs an anomaly score from cached residual and text features; the language model is not used as the detector.

This work is grounded in both reproducible public evaluation and industrial integration. We evaluate on public 5G telemetry, enterprise-scale Open RAN telemetry, and \textsc{LabTrace-SA}, a Nokia collaboration benchmark built from real Samsung 5G RAN lab PCAPs into which SYN-flood anomalies are injected; the captures themselves contain no naturally occurring attacks. The \textsc{LabTrace-SA} data-construction pipeline runs on Nokia's GKE-based infrastructure, and KAC is integrated with this pipeline in \emph{pre-production shadow mode}: it scores cached windows for offline logging and does not yet drive operator-facing alerting or mitigation. The public benchmarks, training/evaluation scripts, and KAC implementation are released for independent replication.\footnote{Code and reproducibility artifact: \url{https://github.com/ChimanSalavati/kac-telecom-anomaly-detection}. The preprocessing schema and public-benchmark code paths are documented in the repository.} Our main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item We introduce KAC, a telecom-oriented anomaly detector that combines full-KPI Chronos-2 residual/uncertainty features with compact operator-style context, addressing two practical residual-only failure modes: context extrapolation and operating-context erasure.

    \item We propose KPI-level residual--text alignment with learnable KPI queries, shared residual projections, and optional uncertainty weighting based on prediction-interval width.

    \item We develop a practical text pipeline for telecom observability, including label-indicative term masking, train-split-only saliency for high-dimensional settings, and empirical leakage checks.

    \item We evaluate KAC against trained residual, deep MTSAD, frozen-foundation, classical, and zero-shot LLM baselines across three complementary telecom benchmarks, report low-FPR alert-budget behavior and sparse-positive confidence intervals, and provide pre-production shadow-integration and CPU-cost evidence for Nokia's KPI construction pipeline (operator-facing impact metrics are left to future deployment).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Telecom anomaly detection has moved from manual thresholding toward AI/ML-based monitoring for 5G and O-RAN environments~\cite{edozie2025telecomsurvey,azkaei2025oranmetrics,kumar2025falcon}. Recent systems emphasize the need to reason over multiple telemetry layers: SpotLight, for example, combines edge-side anomaly detection with cloud-side localization over fine-grained RAN and platform metrics~\cite{sun2024spotlight}. In parallel, AIOps studies identify alert and incident management as a central failure-management task, with dynamic alert suppression highlighting the persistence of noisy alert streams in operational settings~\cite{yu2024alertsurvey,bhukar2024dynamic,zhang2024aiopsllm}. These works motivate practical KPI anomaly detection, but they primarily operate on numerical telemetry.

Time-series foundation models such as Chronos/Chronos-2~\cite{ansari2024chronos,ansari2025chronos2}, MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment}, Moirai~\cite{woo2024moirai}, and TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm} provide pretrained forecasting or representation backbones that can be reused for downstream time-series tasks. A common anomaly-detection strategy is to score reconstruction or forecasting errors, or to train shallow probes on frozen representations; however, recent evidence suggests that such direct reuse can be weak for anomaly detection~\cite{zhu2026oneliners}. Prompting-based LLM detectors, including SigLLM~\cite{alnegheimish2024sigllm} and recent systematic studies of LLMs for time-series anomaly detection~\cite{zhou2025llmts}, show promise on simple cases but remain less reliable than specialized detectors in subtle regimes.

Contrastive learning has become a common tool for improving MTSAD representations. CAROTS constructs causality-preserving and causality-disturbing augmentations~\cite{kim2025carots}; MPFormer uses multi-patch Transformer representations with contrastive learning~\cite{ma2024mpformer}; TFCL combines time- and frequency-domain views with contrastive objectives and gated fusion~\cite{zhang2025tfcl}; and DCdetector replaces reconstruction with a dual-attention contrastive criterion between in-patch and patch-wise views~\cite{yang2023dcdetector}. More broadly, supervised contrastive learning structures embeddings using label-aware positives and negatives~\cite{khosla2020supervised}, while CLIP demonstrates the effectiveness of contrastive alignment across heterogeneous modalities~\cite{radford2021clip}.

Recent multimodal time-series methods show that textual or contextual supervision can improve time-series modeling beyond purely numerical representations. MindTS studies fine-grained time--text semantic alignment for multimodal anomaly detection~\cite{hu2026mindts}; TimesCLIP constructs visual and textual views for contrastive time-series forecasting~\cite{dong2025timesclip}; TS-CLIP aligns time-series embeddings with natural-language descriptions for time-series understanding and zero-shot classification~\cite{chen2025tsclip}; and TyphoFormer incorporates LLM-generated meteorological descriptions into typhoon trajectory forecasting~\cite{li2025typhoformer}. CLaSP and TRACE further show the value of natural-language supervision and contextual grounding for time-series retrieval and channel-level alignment~\cite{ito2024clasp,chen2025trace}. These methods are not designed for telecom KPI anomaly detection over full-KPI foundation-model residuals. KAC fills this gap by combining full-KPI Chronos-2 residual/uncertainty features, compact KPI-window summaries, KPI-level residual--text alignment, and deployment-oriented shadow integration in one telecom detector.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

KAC is motivated by a limitation of residual-only anomaly detection.
Forecast residuals provide strong temporal evidence, but they are deviation-based: a KPI that is easy to forecast can have small residuals even when its absolute operating level is important for interpreting the window.
For example, a stable RSRP series may be forecast accurately both near the tower and at the cell edge, while the absolute RSRP level changes how related KPIs such as BLER or throughput should be interpreted.

KAC therefore keeps full-KPI residual evidence in the numerical branch and adds a compact text branch that summarizes salient KPI-window context under a fixed token budget.
The text branch acts as a compressed semantic side channel rather than an enumeration of all KPI channels.
Learnable KPI queries extract KPI-specific text representations, a shared residual projector produces matched KPI-level residual representations, and a contrastive objective aligns the two views.
Optional uncertainty weighting uses Chronos-2 prediction-interval width to reduce the influence of low-confidence residual evidence.

The numerical branch always receives all KPI channels through Chronos-2 residual features, while the text branch focuses on salient operating context that may be difficult to recover from residuals alone or infeasible to express exhaustively in high-dimensional telemetry.
Figure~\ref{fig:overview} provides an overview of the full pipeline.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

A KPI window $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times K}$ contains $T$ timesteps and $K$ KPI channels. Each window is paired with a natural-language summary $s$ that describes salient KPI behavior or operating context. The summary is not assumed to enumerate all $K$ KPIs: in high-dimensional telemetry, it may mention only a subset of informative indicators under a fixed token budget, while the numerical branch always receives the full multivariate window.

KAC learns a detector
\begin{equation}
    f_\theta(\mathbf{X}, s)=\hat{p}\in[0,1],
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}$ is the predicted anomaly probability. When summaries are generated rather than provided by the dataset, they are constructed from information available at inference time, including observed KPI values and non-label timing or configuration context. Any KPI-selection or saliency model used to decide which indicators to describe is fit only on the training split.

KAC decomposes as
\begin{equation}
    f_\theta(\mathbf{X}_i,s_i)
    =
    h_\theta\!\left(\Phi_C(\mathbf{X}_i), s_i\right),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_C$ is a frozen Chronos-2 residual extractor with context length $C$, and $h_\theta$ is the trainable multimodal detector. The residual sequence has length $T_r=T-C$. The classifier input uses z-normalized residual features, while raw prediction-interval widths are retained for uncertainty-weighted training.

Throughout the method section, $K$ denotes the number of KPI channels, $d$ the hidden dimension, $p$ the contrastive projection dimension, $B$ the batch size, and $L$ the maximum text length after tokenization.

\subsection{Chronos Residual Feature Extraction}
\label{sec:chronos}

KAC first converts each KPI window into forecast-residual features using frozen Chronos-2~\cite{ansari2025chronos2}. We apply Chronos-2 independently to each KPI channel, and precompute the resulting residual tensors before detector training.

For sample $i$, KPI $k$, and residual timestep $\tau \in \{1,\ldots,T_r\}$, Chronos-2 produces quantile forecasts
$\hat{q}^{(0.05)}_{i,\tau,k}$,
$\hat{q}^{(0.50)}_{i,\tau,k}$, and
$\hat{q}^{(0.95)}_{i,\tau,k}$
for target value $x^{\star}_{i,\tau,k}=x_{i,\tau+C,k}$.
Suppressing the common indices $(i,\tau,k)$, let
$x^\star=x^\star_{i,\tau,k}$ and
$\hat{q}^{(\alpha)}=\hat{q}^{(\alpha)}_{i,\tau,k}$.
We compute the residual feature vector
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\rho}_{i,\tau,k}
=
[r,\; m^{-},\; m^{+},\; w,\; \tilde{r}],
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
r &= x^\star-\hat{q}^{(0.50)},\\
m^{-} &= x^\star-\hat{q}^{(0.05)},\\
m^{+} &= \hat{q}^{(0.95)}-x^\star,\\
w &= \hat{q}^{(0.95)}-\hat{q}^{(0.05)},\\
\tilde{r} &= \frac{|r|}{|x^\star|+\epsilon}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
We denote the $a$-th component by $\rho^{(a)}_{i,\tau,k}$; in particular, $\rho^{(4)}_{i,\tau,k}=w$ is the raw prediction-interval width.

Residual features from all KPIs are stacked into $\mathbf{R}^{\mathrm{raw}}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T_r \times 5K}$, where each KPI occupies a contiguous block of five channels:
\begin{equation}
R^{\mathrm{raw}}_{i,\tau,5(k-1)+a}
=
\rho^{(a)}_{i,\tau,k}.
\end{equation}

Before training, each residual channel is z-normalized using training-split statistics to produce the numerical input tensor $\mathbf{R}_i$. Raw prediction-interval widths are retained separately for uncertainty-weighted training.

\subsection{Compact Text Construction}
\label{sec:text_construction}

KAC uses text as compact semantic side information rather than as a replacement for numerical telemetry.
The numerical branch always receives the full residual tensor $\mathbf{R}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T_r \times 5K}$, while the text branch receives a budget-limited natural-language summary $s_i$ that may mention only a subset of salient KPIs when $K$ is large.

The source of $s_i$ depends on the dataset.
For datasets with provided descriptions, we use the benchmark-provided text before masking.
For datasets without human-written descriptions, we construct label-free operator-style summaries from observed KPI values and non-label timing or configuration context.
For lab-trace data, these summaries are generated offline with an instruction-tuned LLM.
For high-dimensional Open RAN telemetry, exhaustive narration is infeasible under a fixed token budget, so we rank KPIs using training-split-only saliency (label-free residual magnitude/variance by default, or a train-only supervised selector when labels are available) and construct deterministic summaries from the top-ranked indicators.
This affects only the text branch: the residual branch always receives all KPI channels.

In a deployment outside these benchmarks, the intended default is the deterministic summary path: compute train-split-only KPI saliency from residual statistics, describe the top-ranked KPI levels/trends with a fixed template, and cache the resulting summaries. If labels are available during model development, any supervised KPI-selection model is fit only on the training split; in a label-free rollout, the same template can use residual magnitude, variance, or operator-specified KPI priority lists. Thus the text branch is a bounded, cached metadata channel rather than a free-form online LLM interface.

Text generation is performed offline and cached before detector training and inference.
KAC itself does not invoke an LLM during anomaly scoring.

Before tokenization, we mask a fixed vocabulary $\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{mask}}$ of label-indicative or diagnostic terms:
\begin{equation}
    s_i^{\mathrm{mask}}
    =
    \mathrm{Mask}(s_i; \mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{mask}}).
\end{equation}

Generated summaries never use anomaly labels or test-set statistics.
Any saliency model used for KPI selection is fit only on the training split and then applied unchanged to validation and test windows.
For reproducibility, the artifact stores the public benchmark summaries after masking, the masking vocabulary, and the scripts/templates used to construct deterministic summaries. For LabTrace-SA, the summaries are cached with the restricted KPI windows; because the underlying PCAP-derived windows cannot be redistributed, the prompt template and generation settings are documented for audit by authorized collaborators, while public end-to-end reproduction is supported on TelecomTS and SpotLight.
\subsection{Text Encoding and KPI-Level Representations}
\label{sec:text_encoding}

The masked summary $s_i^{\mathrm{mask}}$ is tokenized to length $L$ and encoded with DistilBERT~\cite{sanh2019distilbert}; only the last two transformer blocks are unfrozen. We choose DistilBERT deliberately for the applied setting: it is small enough for CPU and near-edge shadow scoring, has stable open weights and mature tokenizer support, and keeps the text encoder off the critical cost path once summaries are cached. Larger sentence encoders or domain-adapted telecom LLMs may improve text representations, but they would raise deployment and reproducibility costs and are left to future work. The resulting token states are projected to the KAC hidden dimension,
$\mathbf{T}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$, and are used both for KPI-query attention and for final multimodal fusion.

KAC learns $K$ KPI query vectors, $\mathbf{Q}\in\mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$, one per KPI channel. These queries attend over $\mathbf{T}_i$ using masked multi-head attention~\cite{vaswani2017attention}, producing KPI-level text representations
$\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{text}}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$.
Row $\mathbf{e}^{\mathrm{text}}_{i,k}$ corresponds to KPI $k$ and remains context-dependent even when the compact summary does not explicitly name that KPI.

For each KPI $k$, the corresponding five residual channels are mapped with a shared linear projection and mean-pooled over residual timesteps to produce $\mathbf{e}^{\mathrm{res}}_{i,k}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$. Stacking over KPIs gives $\mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{res}}_i\in\mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$.

The text-derived and residual-derived KPI representations are projected and L2-normalized into a shared $p$-dimensional contrastive space:
$\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{text}}_{i,k}, \mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{res}}_{i,k}\in\mathbb{R}^{p}$.
These paired embeddings are used in the uncertainty-weighted KPI contrastive loss in Section~\ref{sec:objective}.
\subsection{Gated Multimodal Fusion and Classification}
\label{sec:fusion}

The KPI-level representations support contrastive alignment, while the final anomaly score is produced from a fused sequence containing text tokens and the full-KPI residual sequence in the shared hidden space.

We first project the residual tensor to the hidden dimension,
$\mathbf{U}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T_r \times d}$,
and compute a masked-mean text representation $\bar{\mathbf{t}}_i$ from $\mathbf{T}_i$.
A feature-wise gate
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g}_i
=
\sigma(\mathrm{MLP}_{\mathrm{gate}}(\bar{\mathbf{t}}_i))
\in
\mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
modulates each residual timestep:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{U}}_{i,\tau}
=
\mathbf{U}_{i,\tau}
\odot
\mathbf{g}_i.
\end{equation}
The text summary therefore conditions which latent residual dimensions are emphasized before multimodal fusion.

The projected text sequence and gated residual sequence are concatenated along the sequence dimension and refined with a residual 1D convolution, dropout, and layer normalization. A learnable classification query then attends over the fused sequence to produce the window-level representation $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$.

Finally, a two-layer MLP maps $\mathbf{h}_i$ to anomaly logit $\ell_i$ and predicted probability $\hat{p}_i=\sigma(\ell_i)$. In parallel, $\mathbf{h}_i$ is projected and L2-normalized to $\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$ for supervised contrastive learning.
\subsection{Uncertainty-Weighted Training Objective}
\label{sec:objective}

KAC is trained with three losses: binary cross-entropy for window-level anomaly detection, supervised contrastive learning over window embeddings, and uncertainty-weighted KPI-level contrastive learning between text-derived and residual-derived KPI representations.

Given ground-truth label $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ and anomaly logit $\ell_i$, we use class-weighted binary cross-entropy
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}
=
-\frac{1}{B}
\sum_i
\left[
w_+ y_i \log \sigma(\ell_i)
+
(1-y_i)\log(1-\sigma(\ell_i))
\right],
\end{equation}
where $w_+=N^{-}/N^{+}$ is computed from training-set class counts.

Following supervised contrastive learning~\cite{khosla2020supervised}, we apply a contrastive loss to the window-level embeddings $\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_i$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SupCon}}
=
-\frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}|}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{V}}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}(i)|}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{P}(i)}
\log
\frac{
\exp(\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_i \cdot \mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_j/\tau_{\mathrm{cl}})
}{
\sum_{m\neq i}
\exp(\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_i \cdot \mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sc}}_m/\tau_{\mathrm{cl}})
},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}(i)$ denotes same-class positives for anchor $i$, $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of anchors with at least one positive in the batch, and $\tau_{\mathrm{cl}}=0.07$.

For each KPI $k$, we form a similarity matrix
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{S}_k
=
\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{text}}_k
(\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{res}}_k)^\top
/
\tau_{\mathrm{cl}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{text}}_k,\mathbf{Z}^{\mathrm{res}}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{B\times p}$ are the batch-stacked KPI projections. We then apply symmetric InfoNCE:
\begin{equation}
\ell_k
=
\frac{1}{2}
\left[
\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{diag}}(\mathbf{S}_k)
+
\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{diag}}(\mathbf{S}_k^\top)
\right].
\end{equation}
This encourages text-derived and residual-derived representations of the same KPI to retrieve each other within the batch.

KPI-level contrastive learning should not treat every KPI equally: wide Chronos-2 prediction intervals indicate less reliable residual evidence. Using the raw interval-width feature $\rho^{(4)}_{i,\tau,k}$, we define
\begin{equation}
u_{i,k}
=
\frac{1}{T_r}
\sum_{\tau=1}^{T_r}
|\rho^{(4)}_{i,\tau,k}|,
\qquad
\tilde{\omega}_{i,k}
=
\frac{1}{u_{i,k}+\epsilon}.
\end{equation}
Weights are normalized within each sample and averaged across the batch:
\begin{equation}
\omega_k
=
\frac{1}{B}
\sum_{i=1}^{B}
\frac{
\tilde{\omega}_{i,k}
}{
K^{-1}\sum_{j=1}^{K}\tilde{\omega}_{i,j}+\epsilon
}.
\end{equation}
The uncertainty-weighted KPI contrastive loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KPI}}
=
\frac{
\sum_{k=1}^{K}
\omega_k \ell_k
}{
\sum_{k=1}^{K}
\omega_k + \epsilon
}.
\end{equation}

The final training objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}
+
\alpha \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SupCon}}
+
\beta \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KPI}},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ control the contributions of the two contrastive terms.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate KAC on three complementary telecom anomaly-detection benchmarks that differ in dimensionality, telemetry source, and class imbalance (Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats}). The evaluation compares KAC with trained residual classifiers, frozen time-series foundation-model probes, deep MTSAD baselines, classical one-class detectors, a paper-aligned Open RAN pipeline, and zero-shot LLM baselines. LLMs are reported separately because they receive no task-specific training or threshold tuning.

\subsubsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

\paragraph{TelecomTS~\cite{feng2025telecomts}.}
TelecomTS is a public 5G observability benchmark with $16$ KPIs sampled at $100$\,ms. It contains mixed real jamming and synthetic telecom-rooted faults. We use the balanced split with $1{,}580$/$396$/$494$ train/validation/test windows.

\paragraph{SpotLight~\cite{sun2024spotlight}.}
SpotLight is a public enterprise Open RAN telemetry benchmark with $452$ KPI channels and more than $100$M telemetry points at $100$\,ms resolution. It includes four anomaly families and six pairwise mixes. Our preprocessing yields $2{,}708$/$580$/$582$ train/validation/test windows.

\paragraph{LabTrace-SA.}
LabTrace-SA is a Nokia collaboration benchmark built from real Samsung 5G RAN conformance PCAPs collected in controlled lab settings. It is not production incident traffic: the PCAPs contain no native attacks, and SYN-flood anomalies are injected with realism-hardening steps such as RTT sampling, inter-arrival variation, and header/payload diversity. The same Nokia GKE-based KPI-construction infrastructure used in internal lab pipelines converts packet traces into $64$ KPI windows. Train, validation, and test windows are split by disjoint PCAPs to avoid capture-level leakage. The PCAPs and derived windows are Nokia-internal and not redistributed; the artifact releases the full code path and public-benchmark reproductions, while authorized Nokia collaborators can reproduce LabTrace-SA internally. We use this controlled, packet-derived benchmark to evaluate sparse-positive behavior and to support the pre-production shadow-integration study in Section~\ref{sec:deployment_status}.

\subsubsection{Windowing and Labels}
\label{sec:windowing}

TelecomTS provides fixed windows. For SpotLight and LabTrace-SA, point- or record-level labels are converted to window labels by marking a window anomalous if any constituent timestep or record is anomalous. SpotLight uses non-overlapping windows with $T=64$. LabTrace-SA uses $T=32$ with stride $4$, resetting window construction at PCAP boundaries.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

We report precision, recall, and F1 under an operational thresholding setting, and AUROC/AP as threshold-free metrics. KAC uses the default threshold $\hat{p}_i \geq 0.5$, selected by validation early stopping and kept fixed at test time to reflect a calibration-first shadow-scorer setting. On the balanced public splits, the trained baselines do likewise. On the sparse-positive \textsc{LabTrace-SA} split, where a fixed $0.5$ cutoff collapses several baseline heads regardless of ranking quality, the trained baselines and frozen-foundation probes select their operating threshold on the validation split, the same protocol used for the unsupervised methods. Unsupervised methods always tune a threshold on the validation split and are then evaluated once on the held-out test split. We therefore interpret threshold-based F1 together with AUROC/AP and paired seed deltas, rather than as a stand-alone ranking claim. Unless otherwise stated, headline results are reported as mean$\pm$std over five random seeds. For sparse-positive \textsc{LabTrace-SA}, we additionally report confidence intervals to avoid over-interpreting small absolute F1 differences.

\subsubsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}

We compare against five baseline families. Residual and frozen-foundation baselines include Chronos-2+LR, QR-TAN, and linear probes on MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment}, TOTO~\cite{cohen2025toto}, and Mantis~\cite{feofanov2025mantis}. To diversify the supervised comparison beyond ``frozen-foundation + linear head'' models, we additionally retrain five end-to-end window classifiers with binary labels: PatchTST-classification~\cite{nie2023patchtst}, iTransformer~\cite{liu2024itransformer}, GPT4TS/OFA~\cite{zhou2023gpt4ts}, the supervised ModernTCN classification variant~\cite{luo2024moderntcn}, and UniTS~\cite{gao2024units}. These five span patch-transformer, channel-as-token transformer, frozen-LM with supervised head, modern convolutional, and unified multi-task inductive biases, complementing the frozen-foundation linear probes already in the table. Deep MTSAD and one-class baselines include DCdetector~\cite{yang2023dcdetector}, TimesNet~\cite{wu2023timesnet}, ModernTCN~\cite{luo2024moderntcn}, MEMTO~\cite{song2023memto}, D3R~\cite{wang2023d3r}, Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008isolation}, LOF~\cite{breunig2000lof}, and a simple residual-threshold rule. We also include a paper-aligned SpotLight pipeline~\cite{sun2024spotlight} using JVGAN--MRPI with validation-tuned MRPI. Zero-shot LLM baselines receive the raw KPI matrix and KPI names, but no task training, validation tuning, or detector-specific calibration. All supervised methods are trained on \(\text{train} \cup \text{val}\) and evaluated on the held-out test split with the same five seeds (42/123/456/789/1337) used for KAC and the existing supervised baselines, so every supervised row in Tables~\ref{tab:telecomts_benchmark}, \ref{tab:spotlight_benchmark}, and \ref{tab:production_benchmark} is byte-identical-split apple-to-apple.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:impl}

KAC uses hidden dimension $d=64$, contrastive dimension $p=128$, four attention heads, dropout $0.1$, maximum text length $L=128$, and contrastive temperature $\tau_{\mathrm{cl}}=0.07$. Chronos-2 residuals and text summaries are cached before detector training. We train with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}, batch size $16$, weight decay $10^{-2}$, gradient clipping at $1.0$, and early stopping on validation F1. The DistilBERT encoder uses learning rate $2{\times}10^{-5}$ for the unfrozen blocks; the remaining KAC parameters use $5{\times}10^{-4}$ on public benchmarks and $10^{-3}$ on LabTrace-SA. For public benchmarks we use $(\alpha,\beta)=(1,1)$; for LabTrace-SA we use $(0.1,0.3)$ to stabilize training under severe class imbalance.

\emph{Foundation-model heads.} For MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment} and TOTO~\cite{cohen2025toto} we train the classification head on the concatenated train+validation pool for $10$ epochs with batch size $64$, learning rate $5{\times}10^{-4}$, class-balanced BCE loss, and a validation-tuned operating threshold; the frozen backbones contribute no trainable parameters. On SpotLight ($K{=}452$) we uniformly subsample MOMENT's inputs to $64$ channels (mirroring the published \textit{Foundation\_Models\_SpotLight} recipe) and lower MOMENT to $3$ epochs with batch size $16$ so its per-channel encoder fits the $24$\,GB MPS memory budget on Apple M-series hardware; every other method on SpotLight sees the full $452$ channels. For Mantis~\cite{feofanov2025mantis} we right-zero-pad each window to its native sequence length $512$, apply per-channel z-normalisation using training-only statistics (required for stable forward passes on LabTrace-SA, where raw KPI scales span many orders of magnitude), and train a balanced logistic regression on the mean-pooled embeddings. The same recipe is used on every dataset; only the inputs differ.

Code, preprocessing scripts, and evaluation configuration are released with the artifact.

\subsection{Results on TelecomTS}
\label{sec:res_telecomts}

Table~\ref{tab:telecomts_benchmark} shows that on the balanced TelecomTS split KAC attains the highest precision ($.961$), F1 ($.960$), AUROC ($.988$), and AP ($.988$) across all supervised and unsupervised methods. Among the five newly added supervised baselines, UniTS and iTransformer are the strongest, achieving F1 of $.928$ and $.897$ respectively, with UniTS reaching $.911$ Recall; PatchTST and ModernTCN-cls trail at $.859$ and $.855$ F1 respectively, while GPT4TS under-converges at the budgeted ten epochs. The largest gain of KAC over the strongest residual-only and patch-transformer probes still comes from the compact text branch: on the relatively low-dimensional $16$-KPI setting, operator-style summaries provide operating-context information that is difficult to recover from residuals alone, even with end-to-end-trained patch, channel-token, and unified-multi-task architectures.

Under the imbalanced stress split, KAC achieves the best F1 ($.800$, tied with Mantis$+$linear) and the best precision ($.900$) in the table, while Mantis$+$linear retains the highest AUROC ($.995$) and AP ($.926$); among the newly added supervised baselines, UniTS and ModernTCN-cls reach the highest F1 ($.555$ and $.435$ respectively), still well below KAC. The Mantis advantage on the ranking metrics suggests that frozen-foundation features with a deterministic linear head remain competitive when the anomaly prior becomes extremely small, even though KAC keeps the lead on threshold-based F1. Overall, the results support the central design motivation of KAC: combining full-KPI residual evidence with compact operating-context signals improves threshold-based detection beyond residual-only TSFM detectors and a broad set of end-to-end-trained supervised classifiers, while ranking metrics under sparse positives remain a complementary regime where simple supervised heads on frozen embeddings stay strong.

On TelecomTS, the largest gain comes from adding the compact text branch, indicating that operator-style summaries recover operating-context information that is weakly expressed in residuals alone. On SpotLight, the additional gain from V2 to V3 shows that KPI-level residual--text alignment becomes increasingly important in high-dimensional telemetry settings, where direct text conditioning alone is diluted across hundreds of KPI channels.

\paragraph{Is it just absolute KPI levels?} A natural concern is that KAC's gains merely reflect re-injecting the absolute operating level that Chronos residualization removes, rather than language alignment. To test this we add a \emph{non-language} baseline (``Residual+Level'') that concatenates the Chronos residual aggregates with raw per-KPI window statistics (mean/std/min/max/last level) and top-K train-split saliency features, fed to a balanced logistic regression with no text branch. Adding absolute levels does help on TelecomTS (F1 $.918\!\rightarrow\!.931$ over residual-only V1) but still trails full KAC ($.956$); on SpotLight the non-language baseline does not surpass KAC ($.936$ vs.\ $.949$ F1) even with direct access to absolute KPI levels; and on \textsc{LabTrace-SA} it collapses to $.378$ F1 (Table~\ref{tab:production_benchmark}), far below KAC ($.742$). Across all three benchmarks KAC matches or exceeds the non-language baseline, so its gains are not explained by recovered absolute levels; the margin is largest in the low-dimensional and sparse-positive regimes where operating-context reasoning matters most.

Uncertainty weighting produces smaller gains on the public benchmarks than the compact text branch or KPI-level alignment, but it consistently improves stability under high-variance settings. We therefore treat it primarily as a robustness mechanism and use it in the LabTrace-SA evaluation.
\subsection{Text Leakage and Context Recovery Analysis}
\label{sec:leakage_analysis}

Because TelecomTS descriptions are externally generated from anomaly-conditioned prompts, we evaluate whether the text branch exploits label shortcuts rather than learning meaningful KPI context.

A keyword audit over anomaly names, diagnostic terms, and context identifiers finds no explicit anomaly-type tokens in the descriptions. Moreover, the performance benefit of text survives aggressive masking of label-indicative patterns, while label-independent KPI-derived summaries constructed directly from raw KPI statistics still improve AUROC over the residual-only baseline. These results suggest that the text branch contributes information beyond explicit anomaly labels.

We further evaluate text--residual alignment through contradictory-text controls. Replacing anomaly descriptions with normal descriptions at inference time substantially reduces F1, while increasing the text budget from $32$ to $512$ tokens degrades performance and makes the model largely insensitive to text permutations. If the model were exploiting simple label leakage, additional text would be expected to improve rather than reduce detection performance.

Finally, representation probes indicate that the primary contribution of the text branch is operating-context recovery. Chronos-2 forecasts KPIs such as RSRP with very small residual error, so large cross-sample operating-level differences are largely removed from the residual stream while remaining accessible through compact text summaries. The text branch therefore acts mainly as a contextual calibration signal that suppresses false positives under different operating regimes.

\section{Deployment and Operational Cost}
\label{sec:deployment}

KAC is designed for low-cost pre-production telecom integration. Chronos-2 residuals and text summaries are computed offline or cached, so detector inference requires only the DistilBERT encoder and lightweight fusion/classification heads, with no online LLM call. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:deployment_cost}, the KAC-specific modules add only $\sim$$0.21\%$ parameters on top of frozen DistilBERT, and CPU inference remains well below the $100$\,ms telemetry cadence used in the evaluated benchmarks.
In the shadow configuration, Chronos-2 residual extraction is performed offline and parallelized across KPI streams, while the KAC detector operates on cached residual and text features. Text-summary refresh is event-triggered rather than per-window, allowing language-processing cost to be amortized across long operational intervals.

\subsection{Shadow Integration Status}
\label{sec:deployment_status}

KAC is integrated in shadow mode with Nokia's GKE-based KPI construction pipeline, which builds per-window KPI tensors and cached operator-style summaries from 5G RAN captures. The infrastructure uses Kubernetes-based telemetry services including Kong, NATS, and MinIO for ingestion, streaming, and object storage.

In the current deployment configuration, KAC operates as a pre-production shadow scorer alongside Nokia's internal SYN-flood detector. The pipeline serves KPI windows into KAC, residual and text features are retrieved from cache, and the resulting anomaly scores are logged for offline comparison against the internal detector. KAC does not currently drive operator-facing incident promotion or automatic mitigation.

The deployment evidence reported in this paper therefore focuses on four operational aspects: detection quality across heterogeneous telecom benchmarks; behavior under fixed low-FPR alert budgets; robustness under severe class imbalance; and deployment-oriented cost analysis, including CPU latency and parameter footprint (Table~\ref{tab:deployment_cost}). Together, these results demonstrate deployment compatibility and stable pre-production behavior without requiring online LLM inference. We deliberately do \emph{not} claim operator-facing impact: detection-delay, alert-volume reduction, incident-level precision/recall, and head-to-head comparison against Nokia's internal SYN-flood detector require a supervised operator-facing rollout and are left to future deployment studies (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

KAC improves threshold-based detection beyond residual-only TSFM detectors and most end-to-end-trained supervised classifiers, but several limitations remain. On the imbalanced TelecomTS stress split, Mantis$+$linear attains the highest AUROC and AP among supervised baselines, suggesting that frozen-foundation features with a deterministic linear head remain competitive on ranking metrics under extremely small anomaly priors, even when KAC keeps the lead on F1 and precision. On SpotLight, PatchTST achieves the highest AUROC and AP and ModernTCN-cls the highest precision, although KAC remains the strongest detector by threshold-based F1 and Recall. LabTrace-SA itself is a controlled lab benchmark with synthetic SYN-flood injection rather than natural attack traffic, and confidence intervals remain wide because of the sparse-positive regime. Because LabTrace-SA and the shadow pipeline both come from the Nokia collaboration, we treat it as supportive industrial evidence rather than independent production validation; the two public benchmarks provide the independently reproducible evidence base. For MOMENT and GPT4TS on SpotLight we uniformly subsample 452$\to$64 input channels (the same trick used by the published \textit{Foundation\_Models\_SpotLight} recipe) so the per-channel encoders fit the 24\,GB MPS memory budget; every other method evaluated on SpotLight uses the full 452-channel tensor (UniTS uses up to 128 channels under the same cap). In addition, the Nokia integration evidence reported here is limited to shadow-mode deployment and pre-production evaluation; operator-facing alert-volume, detection-delay, incident-level precision/recall, internal-detector comparison, and structured operator/user-study feedback are left to future deployment studies.

Future work includes controlled operator-facing rollout, structured operator review of shadow outputs, expansion toward natural attack traces and broader anomaly families, and direct operational comparison against Nokia's internal detector under reviewed production telemetry.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented KAC, a deployment-oriented telecom anomaly detector that combines full-KPI Chronos-2 residual evidence with compact operator-style context through KPI-level contrastive alignment and optional uncertainty weighting. Rather than relying on end-to-end LLM reasoning, KAC uses language as a compact contextual side channel over high-dimensional KPI telemetry.

Across public 5G observability benchmarks, enterprise-scale Open RAN telemetry, and packet-derived KPI traces processed through Nokia's lab pipeline, KAC improved robustness beyond residual-only TSFM detectors and was competitive with or better than strong supervised baselines, particularly under low-FPR operating regimes and severe class imbalance. The results further showed that compact text context is most useful in lower-dimensional settings, while KPI-level residual--text alignment becomes increasingly important as telemetry dimensionality grows.

KAC is integrated in pre-production shadow mode with Nokia's GKE-based KPI construction pipeline, where it operates on cached residual and text features without requiring online LLM inference; it does not yet drive operator-facing alerting. Together with the reported latency, parameter-footprint, and stability results, these findings suggest that KPI-aware multimodal alignment is a practical direction for scalable telecom observability and pre-production anomaly monitoring.

\section*{GenAI Usage Disclosure}
We distinguish three separate uses of generative AI in this work. (i) \emph{Manuscript preparation}: generative AI was used only for grammar checking and sentence clarification of author-written text. (ii) \emph{Offline dataset text construction}: for \textsc{LabTrace-SA}, the compact operator-style window summaries used by KAC's text branch were generated offline with an instruction-tuned LLM and cached before training; no LLM is invoked during KAC anomaly scoring. (iii) \emph{Experimental baselines}: the zero-shot results use LLM APIs (Claude, GPT, Gemini) purely as comparison baselines, with no task-specific training.
